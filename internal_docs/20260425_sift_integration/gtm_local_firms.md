# GTM — Local Family-Law Firms

**Date:** 2026-04-25
**Audience:** Internal — founder + advisors
**Scope:** Selling Daylight's firm-side product (Plan 04, Evidence Brief) directly to *local* solo and small family-law firms — not national chains, not BigLaw, not legal-tech procurement.

This is distinct from `gtm_plan.md`, which covers Daylight's broader product (parent + firm). This doc focuses on the *firm-side direct-sales motion in a single local market at a time*, with Richmond / Tidewater Virginia as the v0 beachhead.

---

## TL;DR

Local family-law firms buy from people they know, in venues they already attend, after they've seen the product solve a problem on a real case. The motion looks nothing like B2B SaaS. It looks like how legal-vertical software has always sold: bar association talks, county family-law section meetings, peer endorsements, lunch demos, $99-per-case pricing that doesn't require procurement. We use the founder's Marine + Virginia network and AIRRVA-adjacent trust as the wedge into the first 10 firms. After 10 firms in Richmond have used it on a real case, expansion happens via the Virginia Bar Family Law Section, then Tidewater, then DC/NoVA. The 90-day target is **5 paying firms in Richmond and 1 paid CLE-talk slot booked.**

## ICP — local family-law firm

**The firm we want first:**
- 1–5 attorneys
- Family law as 60%+ of practice (not "general practice that handles divorce")
- Located in greater Richmond, Henrico, Chesterfield, Hanover, Powhatan
- Handles contested custody (not just uncontested divorces)
- Currently uses Clio, MyCase, or Smokeball as their PM
- Has a paralegal or legal assistant doing the exhibit-prep work today
- Open to bar association events and CLE participation
- $200–$500/hr attorney rates

**The decision-maker:**
The attorney themselves. There is no procurement. There is no IT. The attorney either signs up after a CLE talk, or the paralegal does after a recommendation from another paralegal.

**Buying signals:**
- Any complaint at a section meeting about "exhibit prep is killing us"
- Any attorney who has personally griped about an OFW dump
- Any firm that's lost an associate or paralegal in the last 6 months (capacity crunch)
- Any firm with a contested-hearing-heavy roster (custody modifications, contempt, protective orders)

**Disqualifiers:**
- Firms doing only collaborative or mediation work (no contested hearings, no exhibits)
- Firms that bill flat-rate only (savings on prep don't matter)
- BigLaw family-law groups (procurement, won't move on $99/case)

## Pricing for local firms

Two SKUs. Pick whichever fits your firm. No annual contracts. No procurement.

| SKU | Price | Who it's for |
|---|---|---|
| **Per-case** | $99 per generated brief | Firm tries it on one case before committing. Default for v0. Most firms will live here. |
| **Firm subscription** | $499/mo unlimited briefs (1 attorney) / $999/mo (2–5 attorneys) | Firms with 5+ contested hearings/month. Pays for itself at brief #5. |

**Per-case pricing is intentional and load-bearing.** Three reasons:
1. **Maps to billing.** The firm bills the client for paralegal exhibit prep at $100–$200/hr × 6 hours = $600–$1,200. They pay $99 to Daylight, do 30 min of attorney review, bill the client $400–$600. Margin is obvious.
2. **No procurement.** Most local firms can put $99 on the firm card without partner approval. Subscriptions trigger annual budget conversations.
3. **Try → convert.** A firm tries one case, sees the brief, and the next case is reflexive. That's how this category buys.

Free option: **first brief free for any attorney with a Virginia Bar number.** Friction-free trial, gated to a real attorney population. No credit card.

## Channels (ranked by leverage)

### 1. Bar association talks (priority 1)

The single highest-leverage channel for local family-law software is the CLE talk. Attorneys earn continuing-ed credit for attending, get 60 minutes of focused education, and remember the speaker.

**Specific targets:**
- Virginia State Bar Family Law Section — annual fall conference, monthly section calls
- Richmond Bar Association Family Law Committee
- Henrico, Chesterfield, Hanover County Bar — family-law section meetings
- Tidewater family-law associations once Richmond is producing referrals
- Virginia Trial Lawyers Association — family-law track

**Talk title:** *"Modernizing Evidence Workflows in Contested Custody Cases — A Case Study from a Self-Documented Custody Battle"* (Kyle's lived experience is the talk's credibility hook)

**The headline number for every talk and demo:** **15 hours → 15 minutes. $2,250 → under $5.** This is from a real Monument Labs case study on a 1,271-message OFW export across 180 threads. The full breakdown is published at [monumentlabs.io/blog/how-ai-turned-15-hours-of-legal-review-into-15-minutes](https://www.monumentlabs.io/blog/how-ai-turned-15-hours-of-legal-review-into-15-minutes). Drop a printed copy on every CLE talk seat. Link it in every demo follow-up. It's the proof artifact that converts attorneys.

**Talk structure (60 min):**
- 10 min: founder's custody case as case study — pain points named, no product
- 25 min: walk-through of how to organize OFW evidence for court (genuine practitioner content, no sell)
- 15 min: live demo of Daylight Brief on a synthetic case
- 10 min: Q&A, hand out one-pagers with QR code to free-first-brief signup

**Goal:** every CLE talk = 5+ attorneys try the product within 30 days.

### 2. Paralegal channel (priority 1.5 — under-rated)

Paralegals are the actual users for evidence prep. They tell each other about software. They go to NALA (National Association of Legal Assistants) meetings and the Virginia Alliance of Paralegal Associations meetings. They have their own LinkedIn and Facebook groups.

**Tactics:**
- Sponsor a local paralegal section meeting (cost: ~$200, value: huge)
- Offer free unlimited briefs to any paralegal at a Daylight-using firm
- Build a paralegal-only landing page that shows the brief generation from their POV (not the attorney's)

### 3. Direct outreach to friendly attorneys (priority 2)

Kyle has a network. Use it. Not cold outbound — warm, founder-to-attorney, "I built this because of my own case in Henrico, would you take a look?"

**Don't do:**
- Cold email blasts
- LinkedIn InMail
- Outsourced SDRs
- Paid leads

**Do:**
- Coffee, in-person, with one attorney at a time, demo on their laptop
- "Drop your hardest current case's OFW export in and let's see what comes out"
- Follow up with the brief printed and mailed (paper still works in this category)

### 4. Referrer ecosystem (priority 3 — slow, compounding)

Family-law-adjacent professionals who are already referring clients to attorneys:
- GALs, custody evaluators, parenting coordinators
- Family therapists
- Mediators
- Financial advisors / forensic accountants
- Domestic violence advocacy organizations

These people see attorneys at their best and worst. If they say "this firm uses a tool that organized my client's case in 5 minutes," that's a co-sign.

### 5. Local family-law media + content (priority 4)

Virginia Lawyer's Weekly, Virginia Lawyers Media, Richmond Bar Association magazine. Submit a short article: *"Lessons from documenting my own custody case for two years."* Don't pitch the product — pitch the founder story. The product mention at the end converts the readers who already feel the pain.

### 6. SEO (priority 5)

Lower priority for *firm-side* GTM than for parent-side (where it leads `gtm_plan.md`). Attorneys don't search for evidence software the way parents search for help. But long-tail Virginia-specific queries do convert: "Henrico Circuit Court exhibit format," "Virginia family law OFW exhibit," "GAL information sheet template Virginia." Cornerstone articles paired with the firm offer.

### What we are explicitly not doing for local firms

- Google Ads against attorney-software keywords (commodity auction, terrible CAC)
- LinkedIn ads (attorneys hate them)
- Outbound to firm IT departments (small firms don't have IT)
- Booth at Clio Cloud Conference (national, expensive, wrong audience)
- Generic "AI for lawyers" content marketing (saturated, undifferentiated)

## Sales motion (per attorney, end-to-end)

The local sale is concrete and short. Counted in steps, not weeks.

1. Attorney encounters Daylight at a CLE, paralegal recommendation, or 1:1 demo with Kyle.
2. Attorney signs up using their VA Bar number — free first brief.
3. Within 7 days, attorney uploads an OFW export from a real active case.
4. Brief is generated; attorney reviews it (5 min).
5. Attorney decides: (a) "this is exactly what I would have built" → uses on the next contested hearing → pays $99 → repeat. (b) "this is close, but X is wrong" → feedback loop → fix or document the limitation → re-engage. (c) silence → follow up at 14 days, in person if local.
6. After case 3, propose subscription. Don't propose subscription before case 3.

The whole funnel from CLE talk to first paid brief should be under 4 weeks. If it's not, the brief isn't good enough, the price is too high, or the attorney isn't an ICP.

## What kills this motion

- **Brief quality not at "production paralegal" bar.** Single biggest risk. If attorneys try the product and the brief is a 4 instead of an 8, every CLE talk becomes negative word-of-mouth. Don't ship the firm side until 5 paralegals validate 5 different cases as production-ready.
- **Pricing fights.** $99 is the right number; $199 is not. Don't experiment with pricing in the first 90 days.
- **Compliance / unauthorized practice of law.** Daylight Brief organizes evidence; it does not give legal advice. Cover sheets must be unambiguous. Get a Virginia family-law attorney to review the legal positioning before the first paid brief.
- **Privacy incident.** OFW briefs contain content about minors. A breach is unrecoverable. Tighten storage + access early.
- **Becoming a national play prematurely.** The whole point of this plan is *local depth before national breadth*. Resist the temptation to chase out-of-state firms before Richmond is producing referrals on its own.
